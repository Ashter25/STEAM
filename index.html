The Short History of Eco-Enzyme

The origin of the enzyme can be traced back to traditional fermentation methods used in many cultures around the world.A researcher named Dr. Rosukon Poompanvong discovered a solution, which she then called the Eco-enzyme. Dr. Poompanvong has suffered from a blood disease since birth, which made her determined to find a way to help other people. This led to the creation of the eco-enzyme, originally called trash enzyme.

It is later more widely recognized due to a naturopath and environmental advocate from Penang, Malaysia, named Dr Joean Oon. She is internationally recognized for promoting eco-enzyme—also known as garbage enzyme (at first) is a sustainable way to manage household organic waste. While the original concept was introduced by Dr. Rosukon Poompanvong from Thailand, Dr. Oon has been instrumental in spreading the knowledge and practical use of eco-enzyme globally through workshops, education, and environmental campaigns.

Steps to Make Eco-Enzyme

To make eco-enzyme, mix sugar, water, and leftover fruit and vegetable scraps in a container in a 1:10:3 ratio, then stir them.

Once stirring is complete, close the container and make sure it is airtight, and leave it in a protected place for 3 weeks - 3 months. Note: During the fermentation process it is necessary to open the lid once a week. This will release gas from the container and thus preventing the container from exploding.

After 3 weeks - 3 months, the fermentation process will be complete, and the eco-enzyme should have a dark brown color with a vinegar odor, but if the enzyme is black, add more sugar to continue the fermentation process.
